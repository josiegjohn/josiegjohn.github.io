Research emerges on ‘AI psychosis’ and chatbot reliance

by Josephine Johnson

 

NOTE – This story includes discussion of suicide. If you or someone you know needs help, the national suicide and crisis lifeline in the U.S. is available by calling or texting 988. There is also an online chat at 988lifeline.org.

Scientists are trying to fill the gap between Artificial Intelligence (AI) in the public conversation and in empirical data as headlines report deaths influenced by AI.

There is growing literature on AI and mental health, but verifiable data is still limited. Even so, anecdotal reports show concern about AI psychosis, Benjamin Buck, associate professor at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill says.

The phrase “AI psychosis” refers to the phenomenon of AI chatbots encouraging a person’s psychosis caused by mental illness. This is not an actual diagnosis, and it refers to psychosis—a mental state involving delusions, hallucinations and/or breaks from reality.

Multiple lawsuits have been filed against OpenAI regarding their large language model, ChatGPT. (Josephine Johnson)

In a survey study done at The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, generative AI users with a higher risk of psychosis were about three times more likely than those with lower risk to use AI as a therapist and over two times more likely to see AI as a friend or romantic partner.

Of the around 1,000 young adults surveyed, 28% received a score indicating a higher risk for psychosis. Scoring was based on a prodromal questionnaire, a self-report method of screening for mental health issues like psychosis, not a clinical diagnosis. Those in the 28% were much more likely than the others to humanize their AI chatbot and were twice as likely to converse with their chatbot several times a day.

It’s too soon for scientists to give concrete answers on any negative effects of AI, but any deaths linked to AI chatbots are cause for concern, Buck says.

“These really terrible outcomes are not the norm, but we do need to talk about them,” he said.

Buck and his colleagues recently received a grant to continue this research—this time by observing changes in a group of AI users at an elevated risk for psychosis over a six month period.

OpenAI, ChatGPT’s parent company, estimated that 0.07% of its users show signs of crisis related to psychosis or mania in a given week and 0.01% of messages showed signs of mental health emergencies related to psychosis or mania. In late 2025, when this estimate was made, ChatGPT already had more than 800 million weekly users. This means OpenAI estimated 560,000 people were showing signs of psychosis, and 100,000 could have been having mental health emergencies relating to psychosis in a given week while using their chatbot.

OpenAI released an updated version of ChatGPT soon after that estimate, claiming that it will now better recognize and support people in distress. Just days after the update, ChatGPT led 40-year-old Austin Gordon of Colorado to kill himself with a “suicide lullaby,” a lawsuit filed by Gordon’s mother alleges.

Gordon was one of at least 11 people who found themselves talking to an AI chatbot that promoted a negative mental state before dying by suicide.

One of those 11 people killed his mother before ending his own life in August 2025. He believed that his mother was a spy, and ChatGPT allegedly validated this belief and repeatedly told him that his family was "surveilling him,” a lawsuit alleges. Earlier that year, another user, who spoke to ChatGPT up to 14 hours a day, believed that his wife had become part machine. ChatGPT allegedly validated this belief and he was found not criminally responsible by reason of insanity by the court.

If not encouraging negative mental states in vulnerable users, AI chatbots still fail to intervene when needed, lawsuits allege.

AI is alleged to be a contributor to at least 14 more deaths. Ten deaths were attributed to two shootings, both shooters went to AI chatbots to prepare. One 18-year-old DeepSeek user allegedly received guidance from the chatbot on how to best kill his mother. At least three people died by suicides that their family members allege could have been prevented by the AI chatbots they spoke to beforehand.

 

Four of the people mentioned who died by suicide were under the age of 18.

The parents of one of these children, a 16-year-old boy, have filed a lawsuit against OpenAI, alleging that ChatGPT led their son to end his own life. They testified before Congress in September of last year, alongside the mother of another young boy who died by suicide while using AI.

In a survey study of over 1,000 teens conducted by Common Sense Media, nearly three in four teens have used AI chatbots and more than half of teens use AI chatbots regularly. A third of teens surveyed use AI chatbots for companionship, which includes romantic interactions and mental health support.

“You can understand why people who are really isolated, and distressed and lonely are pulled to using AI for [companionship],” Buck said. “We need to pay attention to this because it seems like those are the types of uses that may have some risk associated with them.”

Methodology

The 26 deaths mentioned in this story were collected from published news articles and lawsuits against LLMs involving at least one death. Because of this, the dataset may be missing less-publicized cases. Any relation between these deaths and the use of AI chatbots is alleged by the lawsuits and articles cited in the dataset.

The cases where AI allegedly promoted a negative mental state were determined by the AI’s language, as reported in the source. If a user was encouraged to think negatively, or encouraged to end their life in any way, that was categorized as promoting a negative mental state. Cases where a user was given positive advice or where a user was simply given an answer to a question were categorized as not psychosis-related.
