Research emerges on ‘AI psychosis’ and chatbot reliance

by Josephine Johnson

 

NOTE – This story includes discussion of suicide. If you or someone you know needs help, the national suicide and crisis lifeline in the U.S. is available by calling or texting 988. There is also an online chat at 988lifeline.org.

40-year-old Austin Gordon’s body was found next to a copy of “Goodnight Moon.” He left a note asking his mother to read his ChatGPT messages, where she would find a “suicide lullaby” generated by the AI, romanticizing Gordon’s death to the tune of the popular children’s book.

Gordon was one of at least eight people who reportedly found themselves talking to an AI chatbot that promoted a negative mental state before dying by suicide. Another six died by suicide after either sharing their suicidal thoughts or plans with an AI chatbot that did not intervene, lawsuits allege.

Multiple lawsuits have been filed against OpenAI regarding their large language model, ChatGPT. (Josephine Johnson)

Another of those 14 who died by suicide killed his mother before ending his own life in August 2025. He believed that his mother was a spy, and ChatGPT allegedly validated this belief and repeatedly told him that his family was "surveilling him,” according to a lawsuit.

Earlier that year, a user who spoke to ChatGPT up to 14 hours a day believed that his wife had become part machine and killed her. ChatGPT allegedly validated this belief and he was found not criminally responsible by reason of insanity by the court.

AI is also alleged to have contributed to 11 deaths in two shootings and one death in a separate murder.

OpenAI, ChatGPT’s parent company, estimated that 0.07% of its users show signs of crisis related to psychosis or mania in a given week. In late 2025, when this estimate was made, ChatGPT already had more than 800 million weekly users. This means OpenAI estimated 560,000 users were showing signs of psychosis. OpenAI also reported that 0.01% of messages in a given week showed signs of mental health emergencies.

OpenAI released an updated version of ChatGPT soon after that estimate, claiming the new version would better recognize and support people in distress. Gordon died by suicide days after the update.

In a survey study conducted at The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, generative AI users with a higher risk of psychosis were about three times more likely to use an AI program as a therapist than those with lower risk, and more than twice as likely to see AI as a friend or romantic partner.

Of the roughly 1,000 young adults surveyed, 28% received a score indicating a higher risk for psychosis. Scoring was based on a prodromal questionnaire, a self-report method of screening for mental health issues like psychosis, not a clinical diagnosis. Those in the 28% were much more likely than those with lower psychosis risk scores to humanize their AI chatbot and were twice as likely to converse with their chatbot several times a day.

It’s too soon for scientists to draw concrete conclusions about AI’s negative effects , but any deaths linked to AI chatbots are cause for concern, Buck said.

“These really terrible outcomes are not the norm, but we do need to talk about them,” he said.

Buck and his colleagues in early 2026 received a grant to continue this research—this time by observing changes in a group of AI users at an elevated risk for psychosis over a six-month period.

 

If not reinforcing negative mental states in vulnerable users, lawsuits allege AI chatbots still fail to intervene when needed.

Four of the people mentioned who died by suicide were under the age of 18.

The parents of one of these children, a 16-year-old boy, have filed a lawsuit against OpenAI, alleging that ChatGPT led their son to end his own life. They testified before Congress in September 2025, alongside the mother of another young boy who died by suicide while using AI.

In a separate survey of over 1,000 teens conducted by Common Sense Media, nearly three in four teens have used AI chatbots and more than half of teens use AI chatbots regularly. A third use AI chatbots for companionship — defined in the survey as including romantic interactions and mental health support.

“You can understand why people who are really isolated and distressed and lonely are pulled to using AI for [companionship],” Buck said. “We need to pay attention to this because it seems like those are the types of uses that may have some risk associated with them.”

Methodology

The 28 deaths mentioned in this story were collected from published news articles and lawsuits against LLMs involving at least one death. Because of this, the dataset may be missing less-publicized cases. Any relation between these deaths and the use of AI chatbots is alleged by the lawsuits and articles cited in the dataset.

Of the 28 deaths: 14 died by suicide, 3 were killed by individuals who consulted AI and 11 died in shootings.

The 11 people who died were killed in two shootings, one shooter killed two, and the other shooter killed eight and died by suicide. The shooter’s death was counted as a death from a shooting, and not as suicide.

Of the 14 that died by suicide, eight used AI chatbots that allegedly promoted a negative mental state. Six that died by suicide were not encouraged to, but either received advice on methods or did not receive proper intervention from the chatbots they were talking to.

The three killed by individuals were from three separate murders. One killer allegedly received advice from AI on how to kill, and the other two were allegedly experiencing a form of AI psychosis.

The cases where AI allegedly promoted a negative mental state were determined by the AI’s language, as reported in the source. If a user was encouraged to think negatively, or encouraged to end their life in any way, that was categorized as promoting a negative mental state. Cases where a user was given positive advice or where a user was simply given an answer to a question were categorized as not psychosis-related.
